Electric Fields Worksheet

From gravity to charge — using the sim's own gravity/electricity grid
Open the Electric Fields sim in another tab (link above) and keep it side by side with this page. Each section below tells you exactly which controls to set. Work through the questions using what you observe in the sim — write your answer in your own words in the space provided, then use the button below to check yourself.
Tip: click Show answers, then print, for an answer-key copy.
Section 1

A Planet in Space (Gravity, 2D)

Setup Set Field → Gravity, Dimension → 2D. You should see a planet with field lines fanning out flat around it.
Look at the field lines around the planet. Which way do they point — toward the planet or away from it? What does that direction physically represent?
AnswerThey point toward the planet — that's the direction a mass would be pulled if placed there. Gravity is always attractive, so the lines always run inward, toward the source.
In the Test mass panel, drag the Distance (r) slider slowly from small to large, keeping Angle fixed. Watch the "Field g at test mass" reading. What happens to g as r increases — does it drop off in a straight line, or faster than that?
Answerg gets smaller as r increases, and it falls off faster than a straight-line (linear) decrease — it follows an inverse-square law, g ∝ 1/r². Doubling r cuts g to a quarter, not a half.
Now keep Distance (r) fixed and drag the Angle (θ) slider all the way around, 0° to 360°. Does the g reading change at all? What does that tell you about how gravity depends on direction versus distance?
AnswerNo — g stays exactly the same at every angle. Gravity's strength depends only on distance from the planet, not on the direction you approach it from: it's radially symmetric.
Switch the Test mass "show/hide" control to hide, so there is no mass in the scene at all. The field lines are all still there. What are the field lines actually describing, if nothing is currently there to feel a pull?
AnswerThey describe the field that exists at every point in space regardless of whether anything is there — the force per unit mass a mass would feel if you placed one at that point.
Increase the Planet mass slider. What happens to g at a fixed test-mass position? If you doubled the planet's mass, what would you expect g to do?
Answerg increases as planet mass increases, and it scales directly with it — doubling the planet's mass doubles g at a fixed point (unlike its inverse-square dependence on distance).
Section 2

Standing on the Ground (Gravity, 1D)

Setup Keep Field → Gravity. Switch Dimension → 1D. You should now see a flat strip of ground with parallel arrows pointing straight down — "surface gravity."
Compare these field lines to the ones from Section 1. How are they different — direction, spacing, shape?
AnswerThese lines are straight, parallel, evenly spaced, and all point the same way (straight down) — not fanning out from a point like Section 1's.
In the Test mass panel you'll now see Across (x) and Up (y) sliders instead of Distance/Angle. Drag the test mass to several different spots inside the field. Does the "Field g" reading change at all as you move it around?
AnswerNo — g stays constant no matter where the test mass sits in the field. That's what makes it "uniform."
Section 1 showed gravity is radial and weakens with distance. Section 2 shows gravity near the ground as constant everywhere. These aren't two different kinds of gravity — they're the same field seen at two different scales. Using the size of the Earth compared to the height of a person, explain why the field very close to the surface looks uniform even though the true field is radial.
AnswerEarth's radius is thousands of kilometres, so a person's height is a tiny fraction of it. Over that small a distance, the curve of the (actually radial) field is imperceptible — both its strength and direction look essentially constant, the same way a small enough patch of a huge sphere looks flat.
Increase the Gravity strength slider. Do the field arrows change direction, length, or both? What is this slider actually representing physically?
AnswerOnly length (strength) changes, not direction — the slider scales how strong gravity is at that surface, standing in for a different planet or moon (e.g. the Moon's g is about 1/6 of Earth's).
Section 3

A Charge in Space (Electric, 2D)

Setup Switch Field → Electric. Keep Dimension → 2D. In the Charges panel, set Charge 1 → + and Charge 2 → off.
Compare this to the planet's field lines from Section 1. Same shape? Same direction (lines going in, or going out)?
AnswerSame radial, fanning-out shape as Section 1 — but the direction is opposite to gravity's: these lines point away from the + charge, while gravity's always point toward the planet.
Now switch Charge 1 from + to −. What changes about the field lines? Gravity never lets you flip mass negative — why not? What's genuinely different about electric charge compared to mass?
AnswerWith Charge 1 negative, the lines reverse to point inward, toward the charge — mirroring gravity's lines. Gravity can't do this because mass is always positive; there's no such thing as negative or repulsive mass. Electric charge comes in two kinds, positive and negative, which is why gravity is only ever attractive while the electric force can attract or repel.
Set Charge 1 → + and Charge 2 → −. Where do the field lines start, and where do they end? Why would it be a physical problem if two field lines crossed at a point?
AnswerLines start on the + charge and end on the − charge. If two lines crossed, the field at that point would have to point in two different directions at once — impossible, since the field (and the force it would exert) can only have one direction and magnitude at any given point.
Now set both charges positive: Charge 1 → +, Charge 2 → +. Somewhere between them there's a point where the arrows seem to disappear. What's happening to the field there, and why does it happen exactly between two identical charges but not between a + and a −?
AnswerThe field is zero there (a neutral point) — the two equal charges push a test charge away equally hard from both sides, so the two contributions cancel exactly at the midpoint. Between a + and a −, the two fields point the same way (both pushing toward the −), so they add instead of cancelling — no neutral point exists on that line.
In the Test charge panel, set Sign → + and place it near the + source charge. Which way does the Force (F) arrow point relative to the field line through that point? Now flip the test charge to −. What changes? State the general rule.
AnswerFor a + test charge, F points the same way as the field line. Flipping the test charge to − reverses F to point opposite the field line. Rule: F = qE, so a positive test charge feels a force along the field direction, and a negative one feels it in the opposite direction.
Section 1 asked what g meant with no test mass present. Here, what plays the same role as the planet, and what plays the same role as the test mass?
AnswerCharge 1 (the source charge) plays the role the planet played; the Test charge plays the role the test mass played — a probe used to measure the field without itself being the thing that creates it.
Section 4

Between Two Plates (Electric, 1D)

Setup Keep Field → Electric. Switch Dimension → 1D. The Charges panel disappears — you'll see two charged plates instead.
Compare the field between the plates to the field from Section 2 (surface gravity). What's the same about them?
AnswerBoth are uniform fields — straight, parallel, evenly spaced arrows of constant strength, the same everywhere inside the region, just like the surface-gravity field.
Move the test charge (Across x / Up y sliders) to different positions between the plates. Does "Field E at test point" change? What happens if you drag it right up near or past the edge of the plates?
AnswerE stays constant anywhere between the plates. Near or past the edge, the field weakens and bows outward ("fringing") instead of staying uniform, because the plates aren't infinitely large.
Increase the Plate charge Q slider. Compare what happens here to what happened when you increased "Gravity strength" in Section 2.
AnswerSame pattern: the field arrows get stronger (longer) without changing direction — increasing the source's charge scales the field the same way increasing the source's gravity strength did.
Fill in the analogy, based on everything in this worksheet:
Gravity : Mass : Weight : g   ::   Electricity : ____ : ____ : ____
AnswerElectricity : Charge : Electric force (F = qE) : E — mass ↔ charge, weight (F = mg) ↔ electric force (F = qE), g ↔ E.
Both a planet's gravity and a point charge's field are radial and obey an inverse-square law (1/r²). Both surface gravity and the field between parallel plates are uniform. In your own words, explain why "zooming in" on a radial field close to its source makes it look uniform — then give one everyday example of a radial field and one of a uniform field, other than the ones in this sim.
AnswerClose to any point source, the field lines spreading out from it are curving only very gradually across a small patch, so a small enough region looks essentially flat and constant — the same reasoning as Section 2's "standing on the ground" argument. Examples: radial — the field around a charged balloon, or gravity around any star; uniform — gravity felt by a falling object near Earth's surface, or the field inside a capacitor.
